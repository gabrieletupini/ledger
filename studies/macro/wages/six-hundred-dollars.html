Study·on wages·part I

The number on the cheque kept rising. The life it bought stopped.

On the printing press, the wage it flattens, and the price of a family

Ask a man who was thirty in 1980 what he earned and the figure sounds quaint — a few hundred a week, maybe six hundred dollars by the time the kids came. Then he tells you what that money did: a house on one income, a wife who didn't have to work, three children, a car in the drive, a holiday once a year. The figure is small and the life is large. Forty-six years later the figures are large and the life has gone small. This is not nostalgia. It is arithmetic — and the arithmetic has an author.

I.

The machine that does it.

A flat line forty years long is not bad luck or a run of unfortunate decades. It is the steady output of a machine, and the machine has a name: the central bank, and the printing press it runs.

Start from what money is. Money is not wealth — it is a claim on wealth. When a central bank conjures new currency, by the trillion, as the Federal Reserve and the European Central Bank both did after 2008 and again in 2020, it does not create a single house, an hour of labour, or a loaf of bread. It only adds claims against the houses and hours and loaves that already exist. Each new euro makes every euro already in your pocket worth a little less. That is what inflation actually is: not prices "going up" as if by weather, but the currency being watered down at the source.

Now the part that decides who wins and who loses. The new money does not fall on everyone at once, like rain. It enters through specific doors — the banks, the treasury, the largest borrowers, the owners of financial assets — and it lives its first and most valuable life there, spent at yesterday's prices, before the dilution has reached the shops. By the time the fresh money arrives at the far end of the chain, in a worker's wage, the prices he faces have already risen to swallow it. He is handed diluted money and charged undiluted prices. The eighteenth-century banker Richard Cantillon described this three hundred years ago, and it has had no better name since.

The Cantillon effect

New money is never neutral. Whoever receives it first spends it at the old prices and captures real wealth; whoever receives it last spends it at the new prices and captures nothing. The distance between the printing press and your paycheque is the distance between owning the economy and renting it.

This is why the assets held by those near the press — houses, equities, land — inflate first and fastest, while the one thing sold by those farthest from it — labour, an hour of a life — inflates last and least. Wages did not merely "fail to keep up." They are structurally last in the queue, by the design of the mechanism, in every cycle.

And when the wage finally does adjust, it adjusts only enough to keep the worker returning the next morning. The classical economists had a cold name for that floor — the iron law of wages: labour, bought like any other input, is driven toward its cost of replacement, the least required to keep it alive and reproducing. A printing press tilts the entire system toward that floor, because it lets the owners of capital bid up assets with the new money while paying labour in the diluted remainder. The worker is given, as a rule, the least that survival allows — and then sold, by the very same financial system, the debt he needs to afford the assets the press has lifted beyond his reach. A thirty-year mortgage is not an escape from the machine. It is the machine's most profitable product. That is the quiet form the enslavement takes: not a chain, but a wage calibrated to subsistence and a working life of interest paid for the right to stand still.

II.

The engineer's wage.

Watch the machine work on a single life and the abstraction turns to flesh. Take the most defensible worker there is — not a clerk or a labourer, but a specialist: an engineer, with years of training behind him, exactly the man the textbook swears will be spared.

In 1980, in a developed economy, such a man earned a clear multiple of the ordinary wage — call it the equivalent of around $10,000 a month in today's money. The skill bought a premium, and the premium bought a life with slack in it: the house, the family, and savings on top of both.

Now price his counterpart today. In Italy, in Costa Rica, across much of the developed and developing world alike, a good salary for a qualified engineer runs about €2,500 to €3,000 a month — and everyone in the room agrees it is a good salary. But run that figure back through the same inflation this study lays out below, and it is the real purchasing power of roughly $600 in 1980. The specialist of 2026 has been paid down to the ordinary wage of 1980 — the modest single income, not the professional's premium. The reward for skill was not trimmed. It was very nearly abolished.

Hold the two figures together, because their meeting is the entire thesis. The engineer's real pay fell from roughly $10,000 a month to roughly $3,000 — and that surviving $3,000 buys, as the later sections show, less house and less family than the old $600 did, because the press drove the price of a house far past the price of the index. He earns a third of what his predecessor earned and still cannot buy what his predecessor's junior could. This is not the familiar story of the unskilled being left behind. It is the middle of the middle class — the trained, the credentialed, the responsible — being walked down to the subsistence line and congratulated on a paycheque that has never, in raw numbers, been higher.

III.

The flat line, measured.

Begin with the wage itself, stripped of inflation. The American worker's real hourly pay — what an hour of labour actually buys — peaked around 1973 and has, for most of the workforce, drifted sideways ever since. The Pew Research Center put it plainly: the average hourly wage in 2018, adjusted for inflation, had about the same purchasing power it did in 1978. Forty years. The same hour, the same bread.

That flatness is not because the country stopped getting richer. It got far richer. The gap is where the money went. The Economic Policy Institute tracks the two lines that used to move together — what workers produce per hour, and what they are paid for it. From 1979 onward they split.

IV.

What six hundred dollars was.

Now the conversion the whole argument turns on. Take that $600 a month a young father might have brought home around 1980 and ask what sum today holds the same purchasing power. The official tool is the Consumer Price Index: a number that was about 82 in 1980 and sits near 325 today. Divide the second by the first and you have the multiplier.

So the instinct is exactly right. Six hundred 1980 dollars is two-and-a-half to three thousand of ours. Run it the other way and it lands in the same place: a $3,000 monthly wage today, divided by the ~5× index since 1978, is worth about $600 in the money your father counted. The big number on your payslip and the small number on his are, to the index, the same wage.

The catch the index hides

If a paycheque merely tracks the CPI, it has stood still — and standing still is the optimistic case. The CPI is an average over everything a household buys, including the things that got cheaper. The family-forming basket — a house, a degree, a doctor — did not track the average. It ran away from it. Matching inflation was never enough to buy the 1980 life, because the 1980 life is priced in the items that rose fastest.

V.

The basket changed shape.

This is the part the single word "inflation" conceals. Prices did not rise evenly by 4×. Some essentials of an ordinary life rose far more, a few rose less, and the average — the CPI — sat politely in the middle while the things a young family must buy went vertical.

Read the column once more. The wage rose 4.5× — a hair above the CPI, which is why economists can say real pay is "roughly flat." But the roof over the family rose 6.8×, the rent 6.2×, and the diploma that used to cost a summer's work now costs 13.8× what it did. The only things that became cheaper in real terms are at the bottom — the petrol and, shamefully, the wage floor itself. A worker on the federal minimum today commands less than half the bread his 1980 counterpart did.

VI.

The young father, then and now.

Put a thirty-year-old man with a wife and a child beside his grandfather at the same age. Give each the median earnings of his moment. The cheque is bigger for the grandson; the ground beneath it is not.

The median home costs about $420,000 — nearly 7 years of his salary, and the deposit alone is more than a full year's pay. One income rarely clears it; childcare can rival a second rent. The same degree for his child runs past $11,000 a year. So he has fewer children, later — or none.

The single statistic that carries the whole essay is that ratio: the price of the median house measured in years of a single median wage. It stood near 3.3 in 1980. It stands near 6.8 today. It doubled. Everything else — the second earner pulled into work, the delayed marriage, the shrinking family — follows from a house that costs twice as many years of a man's life as it used to.

VII.

The house unbought, the child unhad.

Behaviour bends around arithmetic. When the down payment moved out of reach, the age at which Americans first buy a home climbed with it: the National Association of Realtors put the median first-time buyer at 29 in 1981 and at 38 in 2024 — the oldest on record. A whole decade of adult life now passes before the key turns, if it turns at all.

And the family that the house was meant to hold shrank to match. The U.S. fertility rate fell from about 1.84 births per woman in 1980 to roughly 1.6 today, a record low. The large family on a single wage — the thing the 1980 father took for granted — has become a luxury good, available to the wealthy and to the indifferent, and to almost no one in between. People are not choosing smaller lives because they want less. They are pricing the life they want and finding the quote impossible.

His wage was a fraction of yours, and it bought a house, a wife at home, and three children. Yours bought the right to keep renting.

The honest summary is short. A central bank that prints is a machine for moving wealth from those who earn money to those who own assets, and it runs without pause. Pay, adjusted for inflation, has barely moved since the late 1970s — the same hour now buys about what it bought your grandfather — while the things that make a family, the house and the rent and the schooling, outran that flat wage two to four times over, because that is where the new money goes first. The specialist's premium was compressed back to the ordinary wage; the ordinary wage was compressed toward subsistence. The $600 your grandfather counted, which is your $2,500 to $3,000, was the richer sum — because it was pointed at a world the press had not yet inflated beyond his reach.

None of this is an argument for despair, and none of it is the worker's failing. It is a structural fact to be planned around with open eyes: the paycheque is not the measure — the ratio is, the years of your life a roof now costs. Read the ratio, not the number. And learn the name of the thing that bent it: not your idleness, not the market's weather, but a press you do not own, paying you last in money it has already diluted. The number lies; the ratio never has.
