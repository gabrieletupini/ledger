Study·on markets·part II

Own everything, predict nothing, pay almost nothing.

On the low-cost index fund — Buffett's case, and what a Costa Rican actually buys

The greatest stock-picker of the last century spent his life choosing individual companies — and then instructed that, when he is gone, the money left to his wife should be put into a fund that chooses nothing at all. This is not a contradiction. It is the most honest piece of advice he ever gave, and the whole of it fits in two lines of a will. The harder question, once you accept it, is the boring one: which fund, bought how, from where — and for an investor in Costa Rica holding an Interactive Brokers account, the answer turns less on the fund than on the tax wrapper around it.

I.

The instruction in the will.

In his 2013 letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders, Warren Buffett described the directions he had left his trustee for the cash going to his wife. They were almost embarrassingly simple: put 10% in short-term government bonds and 90% in a very low-cost S&P 500 index fund — and he named Vanguard's. He added that he believed the trust's long-term result from this policy would beat that of most investors, whether pension funds, institutions, or individuals, who employ high-fee managers.

The know-nothing investor who simply sits still — and pays almost nothing to do it — will beat most of the professionals trying very hard not to.— the Buffett thesis, in one breath

The reasoning is not mystical. Buffett's claim rests on a piece of arithmetic so plain he called it an "iron law." The market return, before costs, is shared by everyone who is in the market — that is what an average is. The active managers, in aggregate, hold the same market and therefore earn the same gross return as everyone else. But they charge far more to do it. So in aggregate, after fees, the active crowd must underperform the people who simply bought the whole market cheaply. Not because they are foolish — many are brilliant — but because the mathematics of the average does not bend for talent.

II.

What "low-cost index fund" actually means.

Three words, each load-bearing. An index fund does not try to pick winners; it buys every company in a published list — for the S&P 500, the roughly 500 largest US firms — in proportion to their size, and simply holds them. No analyst, no thesis, no view. When the index changes its list, the fund changes its holdings to match. That is the entire job.

It is low-cost precisely because there is no one to pay for cleverness. The yearly fee — the total expense ratio, or TER — on a good S&P 500 index fund is between 0.02% and 0.07%. A typical actively managed fund charges 0.5% to 1%; a hedge fund historically took 2% of assets plus 20% of profits. These differences look trivial on a single year and are devastating across a lifetime, because the fee is subtracted every year from a sum that is meant to be compounding.

III.

The million-dollar bet.

Buffett did not merely assert this; he wagered on it. In 2007 he offered $500,000 of his own money that no professional could pick a basket of at least five hedge funds that would, over ten years, beat a plain S&P 500 index fund after fees. Ted Seides of Protégé Partners took the bet. The clock ran from 1 January 2008 — straight into the worst crash since the Depression — to 31 December 2017.

The lesson is not that hedge fund managers are incompetent. It is that the fee structure is a headwind no amount of skill reliably overcomes. Across twenty-year windows, something like 90% of active US large-cap funds fail to beat the index after costs — and the rare winners are nearly impossible to identify in advance.

IV.

CSPX — the example, dissected.

So what does one of these funds actually look like? Take the one you named. CSPX is the iShares Core S&P 500 UCITS ETF — the European-domiciled cousin of the US funds Buffett owns. It does precisely the dull thing described above: it physically holds all ~500 constituents of the S&P 500, in their index weights, and reinvests the dividends rather than paying them out.

Two features matter most. And Irish-domiciled — that single word is the reason this fund, rather than the cheaper US ones, is usually the right answer outside the United States. Why becomes clear in the next section.

V.

What a Costa Rican actually buys through IBKR.

Through Interactive Brokers, a Costa Rican resident can buy two whole families of S&P 500 fund: the US-domiciled ones listed in New York (VOO, IVV, SPLG, SPY), and the Irish-domiciled UCITS ones listed in London, Frankfurt, and Milan (CSPX, VUAA, and others). Both track the identical index. There is no shortage of choice — the practical menu is about a dozen funds. The decision is not really about the fund. It is about which side of the US tax border you want your money sitting on.

Shaded rows are US-domiciled — cheaper on paper, but they carry the two tax frictions below. The cheapest US fund (SPLG, 0.02%) and the cheapest Irish one (SPY5, 0.03%) are within a rounding error of each other; the fee is no longer the deciding factor.

Here is the fork, and it is sharp, because Costa Rica has no tax treaty with the United States — neither for income nor for estate tax. That absence changes everything.

On a US-domiciled fund (VOO, SPY…), two things bite a Costa Rican holder. First, US dividend withholding runs at the full 30%, with no treaty to reduce it. Second, and far more serious: US-listed shares are "US-situs" assets, which means that on the holder's death the US estate tax applies to everything above a $60,000 exemption, at rates climbing toward 40%. A meaningful portfolio in VOO could lose a devastating slice to a tax most foreign investors never knew applied to them.

On an Irish-domiciled UCITS fund (CSPX, VUAA…), both frictions soften. The fund itself pays only 15% US withholding on its dividends — the benefit of Ireland's own treaty with the US, passed through to you — and because the shares are Irish, not US, situs, they sit outside the US estate tax entirely. The $60,000 trap simply does not apply. Add the accumulating structure, which keeps dividends compounding untouched, and the Irish fund wins on the two axes that actually matter for a non-US, non-treaty investor — despite a slightly higher headline fee.

The practical reading

For most Costa Rican investors buying the S&P 500 through IBKR, an Irish-domiciled accumulating UCITS fund — CSPX or VUAA — is the structurally cleaner choice: a 15% rather than 30% drag on dividends, no exposure to the US estate-tax trap above $60,000, and automatic reinvestment. The four-basis-point fee difference is noise beside those two facts. On the home side, Costa Rica's territorial system generally does not tax foreign-source investment income or capital gains — but that is exactly the point on which a local tax professional should confirm your specific situation.

VI.

The one variable you own.

Part I of this series argued that the market's direction is not in your power — it falls one year in four, on a schedule no one is shown. This is the companion truth. If you cannot control the return, you can at least refuse to pay a tax on it that buys you nothing. The fee is the rare variable in investing that is fully in your hands, fully knowable in advance, and fully certain in its effect. A manager's outperformance is a hope; a 2% fee is a guarantee, deducted whether the year was good or ruinous.

This is why the index fund is, quietly, a Stoic instrument. It asks you to give up the fantasy of beating the market and to master instead the things you actually govern: your costs, your tax wrapper, and your willingness to sit still through the down years. Buffett, who could beat the market, told his widow to stop trying — because the surer path was to own all of it, cheaply, and do nothing. The discipline is not in the choosing. It is in the not-touching.

You cannot will the market upward. You can decline to hand a third of its gift to someone for the privilege of holding it for you.

Buffett's instruction was two lines: 90% in a very low-cost S&P 500 index fund, 10% in short-term bonds.

"Low-cost index fund" means three things — own the whole list, pick nothing, pay almost nothing — and the third is the one that compounds.

He proved it with a bet: the index returned +125.8% against the hedge funds' +36.3% over ten years.

CSPX is one such fund — Irish, accumulating, 0.07% a year, holding all ~500 names.

For a Costa Rican on IBKR the real choice is the wrapper: an Irish UCITS fund (CSPX / VUAA) avoids the 30% dividend drag and the $60,000 US estate-tax trap that a US-domiciled fund carries.

The fee is the only certainty in the whole affair — so it is the one thing worth controlling.
